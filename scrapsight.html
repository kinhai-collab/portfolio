The problem. In semiconductor fabrication, a scrapped wafer is not a fixed-cost event — it is a variable one. The cost depends on where in the manufacturing process the failure occurs. A wafer that fails after substrate preparation costs hundreds of dollars. The same wafer failing after ten process steps — epitaxy, lithography, etching, metallization, burn-in — costs thousands. Each step adds equipment time, consumables, and overhead to the accumulated cost.

Calculating this accurately is straightforward in theory but painful in practice. Each fab site runs different process flows, different equipment with different hourly cost rates, and different product lines with different step counts. SAP captures the standard cost of a finished product, but does not automatically calculate the accumulated cost at an arbitrary failure point mid-process. That requires pulling together the manufacturing routing, step-level equipment time confirmations, material master costs, and finance cost center rates — then running the math manually, site by site, lot by lot.

How this tool works. ScrapSight standardizes the scrap cost calculation across sites. It maps each site's specific process flow, assigns accumulated cost at every step based on material cost, equipment time, and overhead allocation, and produces scrap cost figures at three levels:

• Per lot — cost of a specific scrapped lot at its exact point of failure, with full material, processing, and overhead breakdown

• Per site, per month — total scrap across all lots at a given site, broken down by product line and failure point

• Cross-site rollup — monthly and year-to-date totals across all sites, with scrap rate as a percentage of total production cost

Who uses it. An accounting analyst uses it to close the monthly scrap accrual in minutes instead of days. A finance director uses the site and product breakdowns to identify where scrap cost is concentrating. An operations leader uses the failure hotspot analysis to see which process steps generate the most expensive scrap — and where early detection would save the most money.

The AI layer extends this from retrospective reporting to predictive prevention — flagging at-risk lots before they accumulate full processing cost, projecting month-end scrap exposure for finance, and identifying root cause patterns across sites using classical ML on process telemetry data.
