The Problem · Why Mira Exists

Billionaires get teams of assistants
to run their lives.
Everyone else gets nothing.

C-suite executives have people who manage their calendars, field their emails, coordinate their meetings, and make sure they show up for what matters. That amplification has never existed for anyone else — not the realtor driving between showings, not the working parent juggling three school calendars, not the consultant managing six client relationships from their phone. The tools they get instead — calendar apps, voice bots, scheduling links — were built for people sitting at desks. They were never built for the 60% of a mobile professional's day when a screen simply isn't an option.

Persona 01 · Real Estate Agent

She's on the 101, between showings.
A client texts to reschedule.
She can't look at her phone.

By the time she finds a safe place to pull over — twelve minutes later — the client has already booked with another agent. Not because she wasn't responsive. Because the gap between event and response was just long enough to lose the relationship. She didn't miss the meeting. She missed the window. Every existing tool required her to stop, look, and tap. None of them could act for her while she drove.

Cost: avg. $15K commission · happens multiple times per year
Persona 02 · Working Parent

Three kids. Four calendars.
One double-booking every two weeks.
Every single one is someone's fault.

School pickups, soccer practice, client calls, partner commitments — spread across Google, iCloud, and Outlook with no shared logic between them. The coordination overhead isn't a productivity problem. It's a relationship problem. Every missed pickup, every double-booked evening, every forgotten appointment is a small fracture. The scheduling app didn't fail — it just showed a grid. Nobody acted.

Cost: 8–12 hrs/week · recurring conflict · compounding stress
Why I built this

After 127 interviews across realtors and working parents, the pattern wasn't "people need better scheduling software." It was something more specific: the problem only happens when they can't use their phone. Every tool assumed a seated user with two free hands and a screen. Nobody had built for the moments that actually break people's days — driving, walking between meetings, hands full, eyes elsewhere. The assistant gap isn't a feature gap. It's an access gap — and voice is the only interface that closes it. Mira isn't trying to be a smarter calendar. It's trying to be the first tool that works when every other tool requires you to stop what you're doing.

Why Mira · Market Context

Voice didn't fail because voice is wrong.
It failed because Siri and Alexa were built as bots, not agents.

Previous voice assistants were command-line interfaces with speech attached. They had no context, no ability to act autonomously, and no way to complete a workflow without the user supervising every step. The technology wasn't the problem — the architecture was. GPT-4 context windows, 95%+ transcription accuracy, and sub-500ms latency changed what's buildable. Mira is designed for that inflection point: a voice-native agent that executes end-to-end, not just listens and responds.

Failure 01
No context retention
"Schedule dinner with Sarah" → "When?" → "Friday" → "What time?" → "7pm" → "Where?" → user gives up.

Every interaction started from zero. No memory of who your clients were, what you discussed yesterday, or that Fridays are always blocked. Mira holds full conversation and calendar context across sessions.

Failure 02
Suggestions only — no execution
"Find a time to meet with John" → "John is free Tuesday at 2pm or Wednesday at 10am." → User still has to open the app, text John, wait, book the meeting.

They assisted but never completed. The user still did the work. Mira executes: sends the invite, adds travel time, handles the conflict — without being prompted for each step.

Failure 03
Accuracy too low to trust
~70–80% accuracy on multi-step tasks meant users had to supervise everything. A "hands-free" tool that requires constant checking isn't hands-free.

Supervision overhead killed the value proposition. Mira targets 95%+ transcription and 95%+ intent recognition under real-world conditions — benchmarked internally, not claimed.

Why 2025–2026 is the inflection point
  • Whisper APITranscription accuracy crossed 95% under real-world noise — the threshold where voice starts feeling reliable, not frustrating.
  • GPT-4 context128K token windows make full conversation memory viable. Previous models couldn't hold enough context to act across a multi-step workflow.
  • LatencyResponse time dropped from 3–5 seconds to under 500ms. At 3+ seconds, voice interactions feel broken. Under 500ms, they feel native.
  • API cost10× cost reduction in 24 months made consumer-priced AI agents economically viable for the first time. The product Mira is building would have been unprofitable to run in 2022.
  • MCP standardAnthropic's Model Context Protocol became the universal standard for agent-tool integration in 2025 — adopted by OpenAI and Google. Mira's architecture is designed around this: agents connect to external tools through standardized interfaces, not brittle custom integrations.
Market validation — others see it too

Two funded teams working adjacent problems confirm this isn't a bet on future AI — it's a bet on being first to ship the right product architecture in a market that just became buildable.

Key Product Decisions

Three decisions that shaped Mira's architecture

Decision 01
When should Mira act vs. ask?
The hardest design problem in agentic AI: a system that asks for confirmation on everything is useless friction. A system that acts on everything is a liability. Early user research showed trust broke down the moment Mira made a wrong calendar move — even once.
What we built
A three-tier escalation model: high-confidence actions execute silently with an undo window; medium-confidence actions surface a one-tap confirmation; low-confidence or irreversible actions (email sends, meeting cancellations) always require explicit approval. Thresholds were calibrated against internal testing, not gut feel.
Agentic Design · Trust
Decision 02
Streaming vs. pipeline voice processing
Two architectures, one question: how much latency is acceptable before a voice interface feels broken? Streaming felt instantaneous but cost ~$15K/year at scale. Pipeline processing cut that to ~$5K but added 800–1,200ms perceived delay.
What we built
Chose pipeline for launch — but invested the $10K savings into UX buffering: an audio acknowledgment cue plays immediately on voice input, masking the processing gap. User testing showed perceived latency dropped to near-zero. We preserved the streaming option as a paid-tier upgrade path.
Cost · Latency · UX
Decision 03
One model or intelligent routing?
Running every voice query through GPT-4 Sonnet was accurate but expensive — and latency scaled with token complexity. Simple intents ("what's on my calendar tomorrow") didn't need the same model as complex multi-step tasks ("reschedule everything after 3pm and draft a conflict notice").
What we built
An intent classifier routes queries at ingestion: Haiku handles routine single-step tasks (~60% of requests), Sonnet handles multi-step reasoning and ambiguous intent. Result: 40% cost reduction per session with no measurable accuracy drop on benchmarked test queries.

Go-to-Market & Growth

The product was built.
Then came the harder part.

Most AI PM portfolios stop at the architecture. Here's how I thought about getting Mira to the right users — who to talk to first, what to charge, and what we got wrong about where signups would actually come from.

01 — User Insight
Two personas validated. One drove the roadmap.
The finding
Realtors and working parents both validated the core problem — too many calendar conflicts, too many unread threads. But realtors gave us feedback 10× faster. Their stakes are externally legible: every missed showing is a lost commission, visible and verifiable. Working parents' pain was real but harder to surface in a 30-minute interview.
What it changed
Working parents shaped onboarding — they need to trust the system before they'll grant calendar access. The permission-sequencing flow exists because of them. But realtors compressed our sprint cycles. We shipped faster because we chose the right validation partner for iteration, not just the right audience for launch.

The insight: validation speed is its own variable when you're 0→1 with a small team. Both groups remain primary personas — they just served different roles in the build process.

02 — Pricing Logic
Three models rejected. Each for a different reason.
Considered & rejected
✕
Paid-only from day one
Mira requests calendar and email access on signup. Charging before the user has experienced value creates a trust deficit the product can't recover from. The assistant has to earn the right to ask for payment — in that order.
✕
B2B / team pricing
Every AI productivity tool is going B2B right now. We saw clear whitespace in the individual-first market. Teams also introduce compliance and admin complexity that would slow iteration velocity at this stage.
✕
Per-task / consumption pricing
Creates the wrong behavioral loop. Users start rationing requests to manage cost — which is the opposite of the daily-habit formation Mira needs to survive as a subscription product.
What we chose & why
1-month free trial → $14.99/$24.99 flat-rate. The trial removes the access barrier. Flat-rate eliminates usage anxiety. The upgrade trigger is the conflict resolution flow — the moment users see Mira act autonomously on a real scheduling problem is when they convert.
03 — Acquisition
200+ waitlist. The channel that converted surprised us.
Expected vs. actual
LinkedIn content and AIPM Accelerator network generated awareness. But cold outreach to realtor associations and parent community groups converted at a higher rate than any other channel — including warm referrals from beta users. We expected warm channels to win. They didn't.

Honest framing for skeptical reviewers

Mira is in closed beta with 25 users and a 100+ waitlist ahead of March 2026 public launch. I'm not claiming product-market fit. What I'm demonstrating is that I can go from zero to a production-grade agentic architecture — with real design decisions, real cost trade-offs, and real user data — in six months, without external funding. That's the signal: execution velocity at founder speed, with enterprise discipline.

What I'm still learning

The biggest thing I got wrong about 0→1: I thought growth strategy was a launch problem. It's not. CAC, channel mix, funnel optimization — those are scaling tools. They require budget, volume, and a product someone already needs badly enough to pay for. Before that exists, none of it applies. LinkedIn posts don't tell you if your product solves a real problem. Reddit upvotes don't either. The only thing that actually works at zero is finding one person who has the pain, sitting with them long enough to understand exactly why it hurts, then finding the next one. It doesn't scale. That's the point — it's not supposed to yet. I came in thinking about channels. I should have been thinking about conversations. I'm still learning what it means to grow something from nothing, one person at a time.

ImBranding
AI-powered brand voice & social content agent
The problem
Brand voice breaks down the moment AI enters the content workflow. Every model has a different register — Claude writes differently than Grok, Grok differently than GPT. Teams end up with content that's technically correct but tonally inconsistent. The brand sounds like three different people wrote it.
The PM decision that mattered
Instead of picking one model and optimizing for it, I built a multi-model routing architecture — Claude for long-form voice and nuance, Grok for real-time trend awareness and punchy social copy. The product decides which model handles which task based on content type, not user preference. Brand consistency comes from the routing logic, not from locking users into a single model.
